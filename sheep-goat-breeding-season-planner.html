Ranch Reference — Breeding Timing & Growth

Reference notes for a low-input, pasture-based operation in Northeast Oklahoma: when sheep and goats naturally cycle, and how fast they grow. All figures are general benchmarks — verify against your own records and local extension guidance.

Seasonal breeding — when females actually cycle

Sheep and goats are short-day seasonal breeders: cycling ramps up as day length shortens in late summer and fall. Extension guidance puts the natural breeding season at roughly September through January, with fall the natural mating time. Spring into early summer — especially April–June — is the "off season," when many females cycle unreliably or not at all (seasonal anestrus).

Sheep

Often strongly seasonal — cycling in fall and early winter, then seasonal anestrus in spring/summer, tied to day length. Hair sheep are frequently less seasonal than wool breeds, so your Katahdins may hold a wider window than the average ewe.

Goats

Seasonally polyestrous, with peak activity in autumn as days shorten. Meat goats — Kiko and Spanish, with southern genetics — tend to be less rigid than dairy or northern breeds and may breed over a longer season.

What this means for your breeding-season choices. Nov 1 – Dec 15 lands in the strongest natural window — high fertility and female-friendly. Aug 15 – Sep 30 is workable, though early August is less reliable than September. A June–July season is possible only with the right genetics and management — not a safe default. Selection tip: keep replacements only from females that settle in your target window, and the herd gradually molds to your system. For a low-input, herd-growth operation in NE Oklahoma, the Nov 1 – Dec 15 season is biologically very sound.

Kiko & Spanish goats

Slower to market than hair sheep, but hardy and efficient on rough forage.

  • Kiko kids typically gain 0.2–0.3 lb/day; standouts pass 0.4 under ideal conditions.
  • On pasture alone (no grain), expect the lower end — about 0.2–0.3 lb/day; 0.4–0.6 needs grain.
  • Kiko benchmark: ~60–90 lb at 8 months, roughly 0.25–0.30 lb/day averaged.
  • Spanish goats track close to Kiko, sometimes a touch slower.

Katahdin hair sheep

Notably faster to market, and far more parasite-resistant.

  • Spring lambs on pasture alone average 55–60 lb at 90 days (~0.55 lb/day from birth).
  • With hay + grain, ~75 lb at 90 days (~0.75 lb/day); cool climates often top 1 lb/day.
  • Real weigh-in: a twin ram lamb hit 54 lb at 62 days (~0.75 lb/day).
  • Market weight (80–120 lb) usually reached at 5–7 months.

Why your real numbers will vary. The planner assumes constant daily gain, but real gain sags in July–August heat on dormant warm-season forage. And barber-pole worm in humid NE Oklahoma summers flattens goat gains far more than sheep — Katahdins are notably parasite-resistant, goats much less so. If anything, your real goat-vs-sheep gap may be wider than these averages.
